# Player experience - the emotional journey

What the player should FEEL at each phase, and which mechanism delivers
it. Feelings are design requirements here: if a phase doesn't produce
its feeling in playtests, the phase is broken even if every pixel
matches the spec.

## Arrival (connect screen)

**Feel: "this is a made thing, and it already works."** One card, the
wordmark, an address that is already correct (same-origin derivation),
and a quiet "Server reachable" that answers the newcomer's silent
question - is this address right? - before they ask. No marketing, no
carousel. Anxiety removed is the whole job. When guests are off, the
screen says so plainly and promotes Sign in - a closed door with a
sign, never a door that bounces you.

## The menu

**Feel: invited, not marketed to.** Few large tiles, one obvious path
(Private table), one curiosity path (Watch a game - the "I'll just see
what this is" ramp), Rules for the diligent. Greyed coming-soon tiles
are honest promises, dashed and quiet. The menu must always feel like a
lobby of a club you're already in - nothing for sale, nothing locked
behind grind.

## Lobby (pre-game)

**Feel: a table filling up.** Sounds for joins/leaves (a table has
presence), the code as a shareable object (click-to-copy), bots as
honest filler you control. Waiting must feel social, not idle: the
first coach mark ("share the code, add bots, press start") converts
dead time into orientation. Host settings are visible to all - the
house rules are on the table, literally.

## Matchmaking (ranked queue)

**Feel: a promise with a heartbeat.** Queue size updates are the pulse;
match_found is the knock on the door. Never a spinner alone: show WHAT
is being waited for (players) and what changes the wait (the widening
window is honest - "we look further as you wait" is a fine sentence to
show). On abort (not enough joined), the message says re-queue - a
dead end must always hand back a handle.

## Early game & first purchase

**Feel: "I can read this table," then "I own a thing."** The first two
minutes teach by staging: your hand lifts when it's your turn (P2), the
first landing lifts a tile and drains a hairline - the coach marks name
what the staging already implies. The FIRST won auction is the identity
moment of the whole game: the sealed flip, your colour sweeping onto
the band, your chit paying in full - and, if you discovered it, the
rebate chit coming BACK. Ownership must read as a change to the WORLD
(the band is yours now), not as an inventory row.

## Auctions (every landing)

**Feel: held breath, then a fair verdict.** Twelve seconds of genuine
poker: the drain is visible, your bid is private, the abstain is
always available (no shame staged on passing). The resolve is the
payoff and must stay simultaneous - all bids flip AT ONCE, hold,
compare. Never stagger the reveal for drama; simultaneity IS the
fairness, and fairness is the feeling.

## Negotiation (trades)

**Feel: a side conversation at the table.** Private, unhurried
(turn-exempt), revocable. The offer card sits in the panel like a note
passed to you; declining is undramatic (P4 dissolve - "no" between
adults). What must never happen: public staging of private offers, or
pressure mechanics (no countdown on trades - the absence of a timer IS
the design).

## Being attacked (rent spike, takeover, boost trap)

**Feel: struck, and immediately shown why.** The threat curve
(easeInCubic - it arrives without warning), the oxblood flash ON the
tile responsible, the chit growing over the trap. One tier louder for
the victim than for the table (motion-language 8.3). Confusion is the
only unacceptable outcome; a player who KNOWS why they lost $400 is a
player planning revenge - that's engagement. A player who doesn't is a
player closing the game.

## Winning

**Feel: arrival, witnessed.** Recede, stillness, the gold rule, the
winner's seat rising - then the scoreboard truth. The other five
players are the audience that makes it matter, so the win beat is
identical for everyone (the winner is not shown a private party). No
fireworks: composure at the top is the brand.

## Losing (bankruptcy / defeat)

**Feel: mourned, not mocked.** The greyed pawn stays as a monument;
your estate returning to the bank is staged as one grave, coalesced
motion. Then - critically - the defeated player becomes a WATCHER with
full dignity: the game remains legible to them (spectator-grade view of
what they can still see), and the post-game survey asks their opinion
like it matters (it does). Rage-quit prevention is not a lock, it's
dignity.

## Watching (spectator / showcase / future replays)

**Feel: seat at the rail.** Everything public, nothing private, no
pretense of agency (the badge says you're watching; the panel offers
only Leave). Every P2 demotes to P3 - a spectator has no decisions, so
the game should feel like a broadcast with perfect information
minus the sealed envelopes. Replays, when built, are the same feeling
with a scrubber.

## Rank progression & profile (current + future)

**Feel: a number that respects you.** The ladder is per-server and the
UI must say so (a local reputation, like a club ranking board - which
fits the register perfectly). Movement is shown as deltas at game end
(ratings_updated), not as a persistent nag. Future profile evolution
(match history, stats) should read as a JOURNAL, not a report card:
what happened, what you owned, whom you beat - narrative surfaces,
never grades. Tiers, if ever added, get a DDR first.

## The session exit

**Feel: "one more."** Play again is one tap for the same table (plain
rooms); ranked hands you back to the queue. Leaving mid-anything is
never punished by the UI (penalties are a deferred DESIGN decision,
not a default). The last thing a session shows should always be the
next thing to do.
